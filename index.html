Abstract

Hello! I am Postdoctoral Research Fellow, currently working in the Rolf Kuiper's Computational Astrophysics Group at the University of Duisburg-Essen (Germany). I defended my Ph.D. in 2017 at Pulkovo Astronomical Observatory, Saint-Petersburg, Russia.

My research centers on understanding the process of star and planet formation. I use numerical hydrodynamics simulations to study the formation and evolution of protostellar disks, protostars and protoplanets. More specificaly, I am interested in:

  • Formation of giant planets and brown dwarfs via disk gravitational fragmentation
  • Accretion and luminosity bursts in young protostars

    Abstract

    In our study, we undertake self-consistent modeling of a protostar and its accompanying protostellar disk during the early stages of evolution. We uncover significant variability in the accretion rate several astronomical units away from the protostar, leading to fluctuations in its luminosity. Interestingly, we observe that the amplitude of these variations increases with longer sampling periods, owing to the gravitational instability of the protostellar disk. However, upon comparing our model's luminosity variations with observational data from nearby star formation sites, we find that our model's variability is notably lower, particularly for sampling periods shorter than 10 years. This suggests the presence of additional sources of variability in close proximity to the protostar, highlighting the complexity of early stellar evolution.

    Abstract

    New research delves into the tumultuous early years of stellar and brown dwarf evolution, uncovering the pivotal role of mass accretion. Contrary to previous assumptions, how this accreted energy is distributed within the forming object significantly impacts its subsequent development. Notably, scenarios of "cold accretion" reveal striking deviations from traditional models, challenging our understanding of these cosmic nurseries. These findings underscore the complexity of stellar birth and call for deeper exploration into the dynamics of protostellar accretion.

    Abstract

    Delving into the dynamic realm of young stellar evolution, researchers unveil the enduring impact of luminosity bursts on the chemical makeup of stellar environments. Through innovative modeling, they decode the telltale signs of post-burst chemical evolution, offering a window into recent stellar activity. By analyzing spatially resolved CO emissions, researchers uncover distinct signatures revealing the aftermath of these cosmic fireworks. This breakthrough not only sheds light on the intricacies of episodic accretion but also paves the way for unbiased identification of post-burst targets, revolutionizing our understanding of stellar birth cycles.

    Abstract

    Embarking on a virtual journey through cosmic encounters, scientists investigate the dramatic interplay between a young stellar system and a diskless intruder. Through sophisticated numerical simulations, they unveil a startling outcome: close encounters can trigger the ejection of fragments from the host's disk, with prograde encounters proving particularly adept at this cosmic choreography. These expelled fragments, akin to brown dwarfs in mass, carry with them a substantial gas payload, hinting at the potential for extended disks or envelopes—an intriguing prospect ripe for further exploration. Moreover, these encounters may even birth entire spiral arms, birthing planetary-mass objects that straddle the cosmic boundary. However, the quest for certainty beckons, demanding higher-resolution simulations to validate these tantalizing findings and unlock further mysteries of celestial dynamics.

    Abstract

    Delving into the enigmatic realm of Very Low Luminosity Objects (VeLLOs), researchers employ cutting-edge numerical modeling to unravel their elusive nature. By intertwining core collapse simulations with stellar evolution calculations, they illuminate the crucial role of protostellar accretion dynamics. Revealing a nuanced interplay, their findings unveil distinct scenarios: in the realm of hybrid accretion, VeLLOs predominantly emerge as the first hydrostatic cores (FHSCs), while the hot accretion landscape renders them exclusively FHSCs due to protostellar luminosity constraints. In a stark contrast, the cold accretion scenario unveils VeLLOs lingering in the Class I phase, challenging conventional wisdom. Surpassing mere theoretical conjecture, their study provides a remarkable fit to observational data, underscoring the pivotal influence of accretion dynamics on the elusive VeLLO population.

    Abstract

    Embarking on a journey of cosmic synthesis, we unveil the intricate interplay between collapsing pre-stellar cores and evolving accreting objects. Through meticulous self-consistent calculations, we decipher the profound impact of accretion history on the evolution and lithium depletion of nascent low-mass stars and brown dwarfs. Our findings illuminate the emergence of a luminosity spectrum in Herzsprung-Russell diagrams, painting a vivid portrait of cosmic evolution spanning ages 1 to 10 million years. Moreover, our models affirm the rarity of objects exhibiting abnormal lithium depletion, shedding light on the enigmatic Li-excess phenomenon observed in young clusters like NGC 2264. Delving deeper, we uncover the subtle dance between burst accretion, stellar temperature dynamics, and lithium replenishment, offering a nuanced explanation for puzzling observations. These groundbreaking insights, encapsulated in our self-consistent accreting models, propel us towards a deeper understanding of stellar birth and evolution.
